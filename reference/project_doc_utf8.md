<p><strong>CHAPTER 1</strong></p><p><strong>INTRODUCTION</strong></p><p><strong>1.1 Background to the problem</strong></p><p>Tourism across the world has gradually moved towards becoming more inclusive, mainly because travellers today have different physical needs, expectations, and ways of accessing information. In the past, accessibility was often treated as an optional extra, but international organizations have increasingly emphasized that it is a core part of responsible tourism. Over time, bodies such as the United Nations World Tourism Organization (UNWTO, 2022) and the World Health Organization (WHO, 2023) have stressed that accessible tourism is essential not only for fairness but also for economic growth. This is supported by the recognition that more than 1.3 billion people globally live with some form of disability, making them a significant and often overlooked market segment (Kara, 2023).</p><p>Although global frameworks like the UN Convention on the rights of persons with disabilities have helped draw attention to accessibility, many travellers with disabilities still find it difficult to obtain reliable pre-trip information. Much of what is available online remains vague, inconsistent, or unverified, which continues to limit their confidence when planning trips (Kaganek et al., 2017).</p><p>Across Africa, accessible tourism has received increasing attention, though progress has been uneven from country to country. Some nations, such as South Africa, Kenya and Mauritius, have made deliberate efforts to update policies, improve infrastructure, and train staff in the hospitality sector over the years (Mutanga, 2018). Disability rights groups and advocacy organizations have also played a part in raising awareness. Despite these positive steps, travellers with disabilities in many African destinations still struggle to find clear and dependable accessibility information. Most of what they come across is scattered across social media pages, small websites, or outdated listings. As noted by Moyo and Chikuta (2020), attempts to develop continental digital platforms have not been effective largely because they lacked local focus, long-term sustainability, or proper verification systems. This leaves many travellers uncertain about what to expect when visiting different African destinations.</p><p>In Zimbabwe, the government has acknowledged the importance of making tourism accessible. The country’s National Disability Policy (2021) recognizes disability as a cross cutting issue and establishes key standards for accessibility in all facets of life, including cultural life, recreation, and tourism services. The policy emphasizes that places of cultural performances or services, such as museums and tourism facilities, must be accessible to persons with disabilities (Section 3.4). Furthermore, it mandates that buildings, transportation, and other facilities open to the public including tourist sites must be accessible (Section 3.14). This national framework provides a foundation for promoting inclusive tourism. Over the years, universities, disability organizations and tourism bodies have conducted workshops and awareness campaigns aimed at encouraging operators to improve accessibility (Chikuta et al., 2019). However, while these initiatives have been helpful, they have not fully addressed the communication challenges that travelers continue to face. Many operators in Zimbabwe have at least some accessibility features such as ramps, designated parking spaces, or accessible bathrooms but this information is rarely presented clearly or consistently online. As Dube (2020) explains, the issue is not always the absence of facilities but rather the absence of dependable, verified, and publicly accessible information.</p><p>Within Bulawayo, this challenge is even more pronounced. As Zimbabwe’s cultural and heritage hub, Bulawayo attracts visitors to places such as the Natural History Museum, the Railway Museum and sites around the Matobo area. While some local facilities have made improvements over time, travelers with disabilities still face difficulty when trying to find accurate and updated information about accessibility. Most tourism operators depend on basic websites or social media platforms where accessibility details are either missing, unclear, or outdated. Sibanda and Chikuta (2022) note that the gap between what operators offer and what travellers can verify remains wide, resulting in uncertainty and limited trust among travellers with disabilities. The lack of a central, reliable information source means that visitors must rely on guesswork or personal networks, which discourages many from choosing Bulawayo as a destination.</p><p>This situation has practical consequences. Travellers with disabilities often need to know very specific details before they can confidently plan a trip such as the gradient of ramps, whether entrances are step free, whether bathrooms are accessible, or whether staff have disability related training. Without this information presented clearly in one place, many people either decide not to travel or arrive unprepared for the barriers they may face. This not only affects PWD travellers but also limits Bulawayo’s ability to compete with other destinations that have already invested in accessible tourism information systems.</p><p>From this context, the central problem becomes clear, Bulawayo does not have a dedicated, credible, and continuously updated digital platform that brings together verified accessibility information for its tourism sector. Current sources are scattered, inconsistent and lack verification, leaving travellers uncertain about what to expect. The proposed ExplorAble BYO Hub aims to address this gap by providing a web-based platform created specifically for Bulawayo. The platform will compile verified accessibility details, map accessible places, allow users with disabilities to share their experiences, and enable operators to update their information in real time. Its significance lies in offering a trustworthy and locally focused information system that improves confidence among travellers with disabilities while also helping Bulawayo grow as an inclusive and competitive tourism destination.</p><p><strong>1.2 Statement of the problem </strong></p><p>The city of Bulawayo faces a significant challenge due to the absence of a consolidated digital resource for accessible tourism. This information gap creates a significant barrier for potential visitors with accessibility needs, who currently struggle to find reliable and detailed information. This lack of clarity fosters uncertainty, discourages travel planning, and ultimately prevents many individuals from experiencing what the city has to offer.</p><p>This gap in the market does not only affect visitors, it also suppresses growth for local tourism operators. Businesses with accessible offerings remain undiscovered by a key market segment, limiting their potential revenue. This situation hinders the destination's overall inclusivity while reducing its competitive edge in the broader tourism landscape. Therefore, this project aims to design and develop the ExporAble BYO Hub, a dedicated web-based platform that consolidates, verifies, and presents accessibility information for Bulawayo’s tourism sector. The initiative seeks to enhance access to accurate information, increase traveller confidence, support tourism operators in showcasing their services, and ultimately position Bulawayo as a more inclusive and competitive tourism destination.</p><p><strong>1.3 Research objectives</strong></p><p>1. To investigate the specific information requirements and digital accessibility preferences for travel intermediaries and tourists with accessibility needs.</p><p>2. To identify, screen , and catalogue the inclusive tourist facilities, attractions, and services in Bulawayo.</p><p>3. To determine the essential functional and technical specifications of an effective and innovative web-based inclusive tourism platform.</p><p>4. To design and develop a prototype of the 'ExplorAble BYO Hub' web platform.</p><p><strong>1.4 Research questions</strong></p><p>1. What are the specific information requirements and digital accessibility preferences of tourists with accessibility needs, as well as those of travel intermediaries, when planning a visit to Bulawayo?</p><p>2. What accessible tourist facilities, attractions, and services exist in Bulawayo, and how can they be effectively catalogued?</p><p>3. What are the essential functional and technical specifications required to develop an effective and innovative web-based platform for inclusive tourism?</p><p>4. How can a prototype of the 'ExplorAble BYO Hub' web platform be designed and developed to meet the identified requirements?</p><p><strong>1.5 Justification for the study</strong></p><p>This study is strongly justified by its direct response to a clear market failure and its alignment with the principles of innovation and entrepreneurship. Its primary significance lies in solving a pressing market need by empowering a large and underserved demographic of tourists with accessibility needs. Providing these potential visitors with reliable information is a necessity for independent and confident travel planning which would unlock an important economic segment for the city(Makuyana et al 2020). Furthermore the project drives innovation within the local tourism sector through the proposed ExplorAble BYO Hub. This digital solution bridges a critical information gap thereby furnishing local operators with a novel platform to showcase their accessible services connect with a loyal customer base and cultivate new revenue streams.</p><p>The broader relevance of this research is anchored in its alignment with both national and global development agendas. It directly supports the strategic objectives of national tourism bodies while advancing global frameworks such as the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals specifically promoting reduced inequalities and sustainable cities and communities (United Nations 2015). Finally the project demonstrates considerable entrepreneurial value by establishing a foundational and replicable model. This prototype can be scaled to other regions across Zimbabwe showcasing a sustainable and commercially viable approach to solving a common industry challenge.</p><p><strong>1.6 Uniqueness of the study</strong></p><p>This project is distinguished by its specific context, its comprehensive methodology, and its action oriented output, fully representing the module's focus on innovation and entrepreneurship. Its uniqueness stems from being the first initiative to purposefully design and develop a dedicated web-based platform that centralizes comprehensive accessibility information exclusively for Bulawayo. Furthermore, the study adopts a comprehensive and user centered innovative approach that moves beyond a simple directory by employing a mixed methods research design. This approach deeply investigates the specific needs of both the demand side, such as tourists with diverse mobility, visual, hearing, and cognitive needs, and the supply side comprising local tourism operators, thereby ensuring the final solution is both innovative and deeply user centric. The research concludes in a tangible and action oriented entrepreneurial output a functional prototype of the 'ExplorAble Byo Hub.' This serves as a concrete asset that demonstrates a clear and viable path to implementation and commercial sustainability, which is a core objective of any entrepreneurial endeavor.</p><p><strong>1.7 Assumptions of the study</strong></p><p>This research is guided by a set of foundational assumptions that underpin its methodology and expected outcomes. Primarily, it is assumed that the participants involved in this study including tourists with diverse accessibility needs, travel intermediaries, and local tourism operators will provide truthful and accurate information regarding their experiences, requirements, and available services. The study also operates on the assumption that the sampled respondents are representative of the broader target populations, thereby allowing the findings to be generalized within the context of Bulawayo's tourism sector. Furthermore, it is assumed that the digital divide will not be a prohibitive factor, meaning that the intended users have a baseline level of access to the internet and the digital literacy required to engage with a web-based platform.</p><p>Additionally, the project assumes that the current lack of a consolidated information source is a primary barrier to market growth and that addressing this gap will lead to increased confidence and visitation. It is also assumed that the functional and technical specifications derived from the research data will be sufficient for developing a viable and effective prototype. Finally, the study assumes that the local regulatory and infrastructural environment remains stable enough to support the implementation of the proposed 'ExplorAble BYO Hub' and that tourism operators have the willingness to participate in and maintain their listings on such a platform in the long term. It is further assumed that the platform model, once validated locally, can be adapted and scaled to other regions of Zimbabwe and potentially across the Southern African Development Community (SADC) region, given the alignment with national frameworks such as the National Disability Policy (2021) and regional commitments to inclusive development.</p><p><strong>1.8 Definition of key terms</strong></p><p>1.  Accessible Tourism:  A form of tourism that enables people with access requirements, including mobility, vision, hearing, and cognitive dimensions, to function independently and with equity and dignity through the delivery of universally designed tourism products, services, and environments (Darcy &amp; Dickson, 2009).</p><p>2.  Digital Accessibility: The practice of designing and developing websites, tools, and technologies so that people with disabilities can perceive, understand, navigate, and interact with them effectively.</p><p>3.  Information Gap:  The disparity between the information required by a user to make an informed decision and the information that is readily available, accessible, and reliable. In this context, it refers to the lack of trustworthy accessibility information for Bulawayo.</p><p>4.  Inclusive Design: A design methodology that seeks to create products, services, and environments that are accessible to and usable by as many people as possible, regardless of age, ability, or status, without the need for adaptation or specialized design.</p><p>5.  Market Failure:  An economic situation that occurs when the free market does not allocate resources efficiently, leading to a net social welfare loss. Here, it refers to the market's inability to provide reliable accessibility information, thereby excluding a significant consumer segment.</p><p>6.  Mixed-Methods Research: A research methodology that involves collecting, analyzing, and integrating quantitative (e.g., surveys) and qualitative (e.g., interviews) data to provide a more complete understanding of a research problem.</p><p>7.  People with Disabilities (PWD):  Individuals who have long-term physical, mental, intellectual, or sensory impairments which, in interaction with various barriers, may hinder their full and effective participation in society on an equal basis with others (United Nations, 2006).</p><p>8.  Prototype:  A preliminary, working model of a product or system built to test a concept, validate functionality, and gather user feedback. Here, it refers to the functional version of the 'ExplorAble BYO Hub' website.</p><p>9.  Stakeholder:  Any individual, group, or organization that can affect, be affected by, or perceive itself to be affected by a project. In this study, this includes tourists with disabilities, their companions, travel intermediaries, tourism operators, and local government bodies.</p><p>10. Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs):  A collection of 17 interlinked global goals set by the United Nations in 2015 to be achieved by 2030. This project aligns with SDG 10 (Reduced Inequalities) and SDG 11 (Sustainable Cities and Communities).</p><p>11. Tourism Intermediaries:  Professionals and organizations that facilitate travel between tourists and suppliers. This includes travel agents, tour operators, and online travel agencies who curate and book travel experiences on behalf of clients, including those with accessibility needs.</p><p>12. User-Centered Design (UCD):  An iterative design process in which designers and developers focus on the users and their needs at every stage, involving users through techniques like interviews, testing, and feedback loops to ensure the final product is usable and effective.</p><p>13. Web-Based Platform:  A centralized, online application or website that facilitates the exchange of information and services between different user groups. In this study, it refers to the 'ExplorAble BYO Hub' designed to connect tourists with accessibility needs and tourism providers in Bulawayo.</p><p><strong>1.9 Overview of the report</strong></p><p>This report is structured into five chapters to systematically document the project's lifecycle, in accordance with the project guidelines:</p><p>Chapter 1: Introduction - Establishes the research problem, its significance, objectives, and scope.</p><p>Chapter 2: Literature Review - Provides a critical analysis of existing scholarly work on accessible tourism, digital platforms, and innovation in hospitality.</p><p>Chapter 3: Methodology - Details the research design, data collection methods (semi-structured interviews, questionnaires), and the systematic process for designing and developing the prototype.</p><p>Chapter 4: Results - Presents the findings from the research, analysis of the platform's functionality, and discussion of the outcomes.</p><p>Chapter 5: Summary, Implementation, Conclusions and Recommendations - Summarizes the project, clarifies implementation, draws conclusions, and provides recommendations for scaling.</p><p><strong>1.10 Chapter summary</strong></p><p>This introductory chapter has laid a comprehensive foundation for the 'ExplorAble BYO Hub' project. It began by establishing the global, continental, and local context of accessible tourism, highlighting a critical information gap that hinders the participation of people with disabilities. The background detailed how, despite progressive policies and isolated improvements in facilities, potential visitors to Bulawayo face significant challenges in obtaining reliable, verified, and detailed accessibility information pre trip. This problem was formally articulated in the statement of the problem, which identified the absence of a consolidated digital resource as a barrier not only for tourists but also for local tourism businesses, ultimately stifling market growth and compromising the destination's inclusivity and competitiveness.</p><p>To address this problem, the chapter presented four specific research objectives aimed at investigating user needs, cataloguing local offerings, determining technical specifications, and developing a prototype. These objectives were translated into corresponding research questions to guide the inquiry. The justification for the study emphasized its role in addressing a clear market failure, its potential to drive innovation within the local tourism sector, its alignment with national and global development goals, and its demonstrative entrepreneurial value as a scalable model. The chapter also outlined the uniqueness of the study, positioning it as a pioneering, context-specific solution that employs a holistic, user-centered methodology to produce a tangible, action oriented output. The document concluded with an overview of the report's structure, which will be elaborated across five chapters.
